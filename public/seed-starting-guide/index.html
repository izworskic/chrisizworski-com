Why Timing Matters More Than Equipment

Most seed starting failures come from timing errors, not equipment. Starting tomatoes ten weeks before last frost instead of six produces leggy, root-bound transplants that struggle in the garden. Starting peppers too late means they never reach full production before the first fall frost. Getting the calendar right is more important than having the perfect grow light setup.

For Zone 6a in Bay City and the Saginaw Bay region, the average last spring frost falls around May 6, though lakeshore locations can be a week earlier and inland spots occasionally later. Zone 6a Seed Starting Basics

Most warm-season crops, tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, need 6 to 10 weeks of indoor growing time before transplanting. Start them under lights in late March or early April for Bay City conditions. Brassicas, broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, go in earlier, around 6 to 8 weeks before last frost, in mid-March. Squash, cucumbers, and melons need only 3 to 4 weeks indoors; starting them too early produces fast-growing vines that outgrow their containers before the soil warms enough to transplant safely.

Soil temperature at planting matters as much as air temperature. Tomatoes planted in cold soil stall, they sit without growing until the ground warms above 60°F, and any head start from indoor growing evaporates. Waiting until soil hits 60-65°F, usually two weeks after the last frost date in Zone 6a, produces better results than calendar-based transplanting alone. Seed starting in Michigan: the specifics

The calculator above works for any zone, but Michigan gardeners face a particular version of the problem. Most of the Lower Peninsula runs Zone 5b to 6b, and the frost tables tell the real story: the median last freeze ranges from late April in Detroit to June 20 in the northern interior. That range is the whole reason to start seed indoors here. Our summers are warm enough for tomatoes, peppers, and melons, but not long enough to grow those crops from seed sown outdoors. Starting them inside, four to ten weeks ahead depending on the crop, is how a short northern season grows long season fruit.

Match the indoor lead time to the crop. Peppers and eggplant are the slowpokes at eight to ten weeks before your last frost; tomatoes want six to eight; the brassicas want four to six; and the fast movers like cucumbers, squash, and melons need only two to three weeks and resent sitting in a pot longer. The exact dates for your town are in the Michigan planting calendar, which computes every crop from your nearest of 78 frost stations.

Light is the thing most people get wrong

A sunny windowsill is not enough light for seedlings in a Michigan March, when the sun is low and the days are short. Starved for light, seedlings stretch, lean, and flop, and a leggy seedling never fully recovers. The fix is cheap and total: an ordinary shop light with LED or fluorescent tubes, hung two to three inches above the leaf tops and raised as the plants grow, running fourteen to sixteen hours a day on a timer. Closer than you think, longer than you think.

Warmth to germinate, cool to grow

Two temperatures matter. Most seeds germinate fastest with warm soil, 70 to 75 degrees, which usually means a heat mat under the tray because our basements run colder in early spring. Once the seedlings are up, they grow sturdier a little cooler, in the mid 60s, which keeps them compact instead of soft. Warm feet to sprout, cool air to grow.

Harden off, or lose the crop in a weekend

Seedlings raised indoors have never felt wind, direct sun, or a cold night, and moving them straight to the garden shocks them badly. Harden off over seven to ten days: start with an hour outdoors in shade and shelter, add time and exposure each day, bring them in on cold nights, then transplant. Skipping this step is the most common way a whole flat of healthy starts dies in the first weekend out.
